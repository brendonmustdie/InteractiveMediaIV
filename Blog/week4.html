I love internet artwork! I think the definition is a little bit slippery to me, and I have a hard time grasping all the different definitions of it. But so far, so good - I really enjoy artworks that scare users. The way we use the internet right now is too lax for what's possible to be done. There are voice recorders that can impersonate you. Deepfakes are scary as hell. AI generated content changes the precedent of video or even photo evidence. Using the same password for everything puts you at tremendous risk - it reveals ALL of your information, making you an easy target for fraud, or scams.

The internet is a beautiful, terrifying place. And I really love the versatility of the medium. I'm also enjoying how much the concept of "the medium is the message" is unfolding throughout the course. Every other day it resonates with me in a new way. I have yet to find an internet artwork I didn't like.

"Park Design Principles for Interactive User Experience Through Net Art" really intrested me. The idea of interactivity in art adds so many layers to every artwork, both as an artist and a viewer. The section on collaborative participation made me think about this one South African artwork.

WIllem Boshoff created "Blind Alphabet", in which he created 340 wooden carved objects, covering them in braille. To "view" the artwork you have to be blind, or have a blind person with you, who is willing to read the braille for you. It's an interesting role-reversal that requires the seeing to rely on the blind for descriptions. This artwork, which I saw in high school, was really my first introduction to interactive work. And it led me here.

So I'm happy to come full circle with this reading, which reminds me of it.

Steven Mailloux's "Convention and Context" shines the light between the afformention in literature - he explains that conventions are changed by the real word in which they are interacted with and read. I guess it explains why there are different conventions for different genres, and also across different times. This is stupid, but I've started reading some of the books off my list and I'm noticing that in Russian novels in particular ("Crime and Punishment" by Dostoeyvsky and "War and Peace" by Tolstoy, specifically) have a different convention than most english books in the way of dialogue.

"Crime and Punishment" more noticeably, has pages and pages that are just ONE man talking. English novels kind of require a back and forth dialogue. I'm rambling now but it's a little bit like this.

A Russian novel:

"Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit, sed do eiusmod tempor incididunt ut labore et dolore magna aliqua." He said, "Ut enim ad minim veniam, quis nostrud exercitation ullamco laboris nisi ut aliquip ex ea commodo consequat. Duis aute irure dolor in reprehenderit in voluptate velit esse cillum dolore eu fugiat nulla pariatur." He downed his beer, "Excepteur sint occaecat cupidatat non proident, sunt in culpa qui officia deserunt mollit anim id est laborum.

An English novel:

"Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit, sed do eiusmod tempor incididunt ut labore et dolore magna aliqua." She said to him.
"Ut enim ad minim veniam, quis nostrud exercitation ullamco laboris nisi ut aliquip ex ea commodo consequat." He laughed in reply, his eyes twinkling.

The best point Mailloux makes is that these conventions are not neutral, and cannot be interpreted that way, because there are cultural and historical contexts for everything that gets read. Our understanding of "Crime and Punishment" is dictated by our context of, ironically, crime and punishment. Though the context that the author had, is different from ours.

That's why it's so interesting to read books written in different times: the conventions are reflections of the context that they are written in.

I spoke a lot about my connection with internet art in last week's blog post, but I'll ramble a bit more here, too. This week I started to really brainstorm for my artwork. You can find my brainstorming, prep work, and research if you click here.

I briefly mentioned a lot of my favourite artworks there, but I'll recap one: Google Alarm (Jamie Dubs, Google Alarm, 2010) is probably the best use. I'm a little upset that I didn't think of it myself.

Quick overview: Google Alarm is a chrome extension that will set of an alarm everytime your data is collected by google. It rings a lot.

I connected so deeply with this artwork because it speaks to privacy (which is really important to me) in a way that raises awareness to the average user. It's really important to know what about you is being collected, and what's being sold. So you can analyze how many decisions you make are actually yours.

I'm going to get a litte philosophical about it and bring up the concept of free will - arguing among each other about which choices we make is a waste of time when a large portion of our choices (what to buy, what to eat, when to eat, how much to weigh, who to vote for, who to date, where we shop, when we shop, what we think) is controlled by ultra-mega corporations by Google. Thomas Hobbes and related philosophers argue we have absolute freedom - but how can we, when so many decisions are being made for us?
